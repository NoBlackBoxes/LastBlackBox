# Teaching Guide

## Topics
- Atoms — what are they? How do we organise them?
- Structure of atoms
- The Periodic Table
- Basics of electronics
- Voltage, Current, Resistance
- Ohm's Law
- Conductors vs insulators
- Voltage dividers
- Transducers

## Goal
Make measurements and build a light-sensitive circuit.
- Understand atoms, the periodic table, and why some elements conduct
- Explain voltage, current, and resistance, and use Ohm's Law
- Measure battery voltage and resistor values with a multimeter
- Build a circuit whose output voltage changes with light (LDR)

## Tools
- Balloons (static electricity / voltage) - *optional*
- Periodic Table (conductors)
- Multimeter

## Steps
> lecture - **practical** - *optional*

**Part 1 — Atoms**

- Session outline (morning): Part 1, Part 2, Practicals
- Atoms — what are atoms? (Feynman: atomic hypothesis)
- Structure of an atom — nucleus (protons, neutrons), electrons on shells (classical view)
- Electric force: atoms held together by attraction between + and − charges
- Number of protons determines atom type, ~number of electrons, chemical elements
- Organising elements — by atomic number (e.g. helium: 2 protons, one full shell)
- Does it get simpler? — hydrogen
- The periodic table — groups, periods, # shells
- Example atom (e.g. oxygen: N=8, P=8, e⁻=8) — orbits and valence electrons
- Valence electrons: only the outer orbit interacts with other electrons
- Almost-full orbits tend to acquire electrons (e.g. oxygen); almost-empty tend to give them away (e.g. lithium)
- Periodic table — # valence electrons by group
- Properties of elements — where is the shiniest group?
- Conductors

**Part 2 — Electronics**

- Basics of electronics — most materials are electrically neutral
- Can we create an imbalance of electrons or protons?
+ *Balloon experiment*
- Static electricity — imbalance of charge (balloons: negative vs relative positive)
- Voltage — difference in charge concentration between areas
- Voltage — imbalance drives equalisation; movement of charge; can do useful work
- How can we generate stable voltages?
- Batteries
+ **Measure the voltage of your battery**
- Conductors — how can electrons (charge) move between areas?
- Materials that allow electrons to move between them; copper wire
- Current — quantifying movement of charge in a conductor (charges/second → Amps; 1 e⁻ ≈ 10⁻¹⁹ C)
- Conventional current direction is opposite to electron flow
- Resistors — do all materials conduct electricity?
- Insulators (e.g. silicon, oxygen)
- Resistors — mixture of metals and ceramics; regulate how many electrons pass through the circuit
- Resistor key points: resistance in Ω, series/parallel, colour codes, heat, maximum voltage limit
+ **Measure resistance**
- Ohm's Law
- Ohm's Law example (e.g. V = 10, R = 0.001) — does it always apply?
- Voltage dividers — what are they used for? Current and voltage at points A and B
- Transducers — convert one form of energy into another
- Transducer examples: piezo (pressure → voltage), photodiode (light → current), thermistor (heat → resistance), LDR (light → resistance)
- Light-dependent resistor (LDR)
+ **Build a light-sensitive circuit**
